Climate Change and what we can do about it


Climate can be referred to as a statistical description in terms of the mean and variability of relevant quantities over a period ranging from months to thousands to millions of years. While any individual extreme event cannot be causally linked to climate change, there could be a higher probability of more severe extreme events in the future. There are several potential scenarios in which more heating, less cooling and less fluctuations between the extremes with different forms of distributional shifts in climatic conditions, all having the same change in the mean temperature. These changes in the distribution of climatic conditions may have an impact on agriculture, on different regions.



Climate is affected by a combination of factors from three different systems, that is solar system, earth system and earth’s dynamics. The earth’s solar system has an impact on the Earth’s System composed of five components:

  • Atmosphere
  • Hydrosphere
  • Biosphere
  • Geosphere
  • Cryosphere





Rainfall has a positive effect on revenues from all crops, temperature has a negative effect on all crops. With exception from tea, Tea does well in monsoon lands where high temperatures are encountered, long growing seasons and heavy rainfall for effective growth of tea bushes. A temperature of 21°c during growing is required for not less than 8 months.



Climate has a non-linear relationship with revenue from all crops, maize and tea. The coefficients associated with temperature are much larger than those for rainfall, thus temperature has a greater impact on production compared to rainfall. This confirms the findings that temperature is a more important contributor to climate change impact than rainfall and that temperatures will be a significant factor for Kenyan crop yields in future. Previous studies such as Monteith clearly indicate that the amount of water required for plant development increases with temperature, implying a positive rather than negative interaction. With this kind of scenario, we conclude that, with smallholder farm level data, there is a climate change effect on crop and maize revenue without a strong interaction between rainfall and temperature.



It is becoming a destroyed world in terms of climatic conditions and for this reason we need to have a different approach in how we deal with the earth.
